02 — Understanding Your Operation

Before I propose changes, here is what I see about your business.

Anyone can read a job description. Fewer take the time to map the operation underneath it. These are the realities I've taken from your post and your website — and they shape every commitment that follows.

01

You're not running one business. You're running four.

UK new-build sales. Dubai off-plan. CasaCity lettings. Cross-sell into mortgages, solicitors, valuations, snagging, and renovations. Each has its own pipeline, its own paperwork rhythm, its own stakeholders. Without a tight operational hub, the seams between them are exactly where deals leak.

02

Speed and accuracy are in tension — and you need both.

A property consultancy moves at the pace of the slowest stakeholder: a solicitor who hasn't replied, a developer who's late on a stock list, a CRM record that's missing a phone number. The role you're hiring for is the function that closes those gaps before they become escalations.

03

The CEO's time is your scarcest resource.

Your post makes this clear: nothing should fall through the cracks, but the CEO shouldn't be the one watching for them either. The job is to be a filter — a smart one — so the CEO sees what genuinely needs them and nothing else.

04

You're scaling, and the back office must scale faster.

Two consecutive International Property Awards, expansion into Dubai, a growing introducer network. Growth exposes operational weakness fastest. The role I'm proposing isn't to run alongside that growth — it's to get ahead of it.

03 — The 90-Day Plan

A clear path from day one. Measured, not promised.

Here is exactly what I commit to delivering across the first three months. Not aspirations — concrete outputs you can hold me to in our 30-day check-in.

Phase One · Foundation Days 1 — 30

Learn the operation. Stabilise the basics.

The first month is not for making changes. It is for understanding the business well enough to make the right ones in months two and three.

  • CEO inbox under control. Triage system in place. A consistent daily brief landing before the UK day starts so leadership opens the laptop to clarity, not chaos.
  • Calendar and meeting hygiene. Every meeting has an agenda or it doesn't happen. Buffers between calls. Travel and accommodation arrangements handled without prompting.
  • HubSpot audit complete. A written one-page report of what's clean, what's drifting, what's missing — and a recommendation on what to fix first.
  • Portfolio map. A single-source overview of all 60+ UK developments and the Dubai portfolio: status, key contacts, last-updated dates, where the data lives.
  • Stakeholder directory. Developer partners, agents, introducers, solicitors — names, roles, preferred contact, and the relationship history I can find. So I'm useful from week three.
  • Process gaps logged. A running list of frictions I've spotted, kept private until I have something specific to recommend.
Phase Two · Systems Days 31 — 60

Tighten the back office. Take work off the team.

By day 31, the inbox and calendar should be running without you thinking about them. Now we move to the operational layer underneath.

  • Property portal accuracy. Pricing, availability, unit details, and media verified against developer source data on a weekly cadence. Rightmove and other listings reflect reality, not a snapshot from three weeks ago.
  • HubSpot brought up to standard. Pipeline stages applied consistently across active deals. Contact records deduplicated and tagged. Reporting dashboards rebuilt to show leadership what they actually need to see.
  • Document templates audited and refreshed. Reservation forms, agency agreements, commission letters, sales memoranda, investment brochures, one-pagers — current branding, current language, version-controlled.
  • Deal progression tracker live. Every active sale visible in one view: stage, next action, owner, blocker, last contact. The CEO can see the entire pipeline in 60 seconds.
  • Commission log reconciled. Outstanding invoices identified, follow-ups initiated, the log brought to a clean baseline so we never have to ask "did this get paid?" again.
  • Onboarding flow documented. A repeatable checklist for bringing on new agents and introducers, so the next ten join the same way as the first.
Phase Three · Leverage Days 61 — 90

Move from operator to multiplier.

By day 60, the basics run themselves. The final phase is about creating leverage — making each member of the team measurably more productive.

  • Weekly leadership report standardised. Active deals, pipeline movement, outstanding commissions, stakeholder activity, risks — delivered every Monday before the UK day starts.
  • Monthly business review prepared. A clean, narrative-led summary the CEO can take into investor or partner conversations without rebuilding from scratch.
  • Shared file system reorganised. Development folders, marketing assets, signed agreements, investor documents — locatable in three clicks or fewer, by anyone on the team.
  • AI-assisted drafting workflow established. Templates and prompts that let me produce first drafts of correspondence in minutes, not hours, while preserving the CEO's voice.
  • First proactive recommendations delivered. Three to five specific, costed suggestions for tightening the operation further — based on what I've actually observed, not what looked good on the job description.
  • Day 90 check-in. Honest review against this plan. What worked. What didn't. What we change next.
04 — What This Looks Like, Concretely

A before-and-after of your CEO's day.

The clearest way to describe what changes is to describe what stops happening.

  • Commission log is reconciled weekly. Nothing is ever a surprise.
  • The CEO becomes the strategist again — because operations no longer needs the CEO to function.
05 — How I Operate

A short manifesto, so you know what you're hiring.

Process matters. So does temperament. These are the principles I bring to the work, in plain language.

Confidentiality is binary.

Either everything is treated as confidential by default, or nothing meaningfully is. I default to silence unless I have explicit permission to share. The CEO's inbox is a vault, not a notebook.

Acknowledge before action.

If something needs an hour, I'll send a thirty-second acknowledgement first. People should never wonder whether their message reached me. That single habit removes the majority of follow-up friction.

Catch errors before they ship.

The job is not to be busy. The job is to be the last set of eyes before something leaves the building. I treat the small voice that says "this doesn't feel right" as the most important signal in my workflow.

AI as a co-pilot, never a replacement.

I use modern tools daily — for first drafts, summarising long threads, structuring messy data. I never let AI send unsupervised, and I never paste confidential business information into public tools.

Document as I go.

If I do something twice, the third time has a checklist. If I do it five times, it has a template. The goal is that someone could step into my role and not lose two weeks figuring out where the bodies are buried.

Surface problems early.

I'd rather flag at hour two that something will take six hours, than stay silent and miss a deadline at hour five. Calm honesty saves more time than heroic effort.

A long-term view, not a contractor's view.

Where I see this role going.
